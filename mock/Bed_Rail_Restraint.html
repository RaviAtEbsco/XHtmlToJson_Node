Bed Rail Restraint for Older Adults

Purpose

Raising all bed rails (2 full bed rails or 4 side rails), also referred to as side rails, on a bed is considered a physical restraint. The use of bed rails is intended to reduce risk for falls and subsequent injury, and to prevent the patient from getting out of bed when medically contraindicated.

Red Flags

  • Bed rails are associated with an increased risk for falls and mortality
  • Use of bed rails is risky, especially if the patient is elderly or disoriented
  • Bed rail restraints can only be used under the orders of a treating clinician and according to unit or healthcare facility protocol
  • 1:1 staffing can be necessary even during restraint use, depending on the patient’s condition and mental status

Procedure

Supplies

  • Non-sterile gloves and additional personal protective equipment (PPE) if exposure to body fluids is anticipated
  • Facility-approved pain assessment tool
  • Analgesic medication, if prescribed, and means for its administration
  • Equipment for monitoring vital signs
  • Restraint device as appropriate per facility protocol and orders of the treating clinician

Pre-procedure Steps

  1. Review the facility/unit protocol for using bed rails
  2. Review the treating clinician’s orders for bed rails
  3. Verify that the order has been recently written or renewed, as appropriate, and that the patient is still exhibiting the behaviors or needs that precipitated the bed rail restraint order
  4. Note the type of restraint to be used, site of application, indications for use, and duration of use
  5. Review the Patient’s medical history/medical record
  6. Review the Bed rail safety guidelines
  7. Follow the Standard pre-procedure steps

Procedure Steps

Applying Restraints in Adults: General Overview. A general overview of restraint use in adults is provided, including assessment information.

  1. Assist the patient to a comfortable position
  2. Be sure that the bed is in the lowest position possible and that the rails are raised and locked securely in place
  3. Fill the space between the bed rails and mattress and between the headboard and mattress with blankets or pillows, to prevent the patient from falling into the space. Older adults who are physically small and patients who have agitation or delirium may be at increased risk for entrapment
  4. Confirm that bed rails are returned to the proper position following each patient care activity

Bed Rail Application-Using All Rails

Post-Procedure Steps

  1. Follow the Standard post-procedure steps
  2. Evaluate the effectiveness of the restraint in appropriately curtailing the patient’s movement
  3. Assess the patient’s overall comfort level, including if he or she is in pain and if he or she is breathing comfortably
  4. Ask the patient directly if the patient can communicate, or ask the opinion of family members, if present. Use a facility-approved pain assessment tool appropriate for the patient
  5. In general, a treating clinician must make a face-to-face assessment of the patient and his or her condition within 1 hour of application of the restraint.
  6. Review more about Involving the clinician
  7. The appropriateness of restraint use is serially evaluated. The patient’s activity level and behavior are monitored such that clinicians can assess if conditions that made restraint necessary are still present.
  8. Review more about Ongoing assessment

Patient/Family Education

  • Notify family members that bed rail restraints are in use
  • Utilize appropriate communication techniques in order to identify and meet the unique needs of this patient population and to facilitate a better understanding of the purpose and intent of physical restraints.>
  • Review more about Communication techniques
  • Family members who are present during use of bed rail restraints should be educated about how and why bed rails are used, prior strategies tried in order to avoid the use of bed rail restraints, and interventions that are initiated to promote patient comfort and safety while restrained
  • Clarify that bed rail restraints are used temporarily only after other options for protecting the safety of the patient and/or healthcare personnel have proven ineffective, and will be removed as soon as there are indications that restraint is no longer needed
  • Caution family members against removing or repositioning the bed rails
  • Explain that having a family member sit with the patient may alleviate the need for bed rail restraint, depending on the indications for restraint

Documentation

Update the patient’s plan of care and medical record, as appropriate:
  • Date and time the restraint was applied
  • Type of restraint applied and how the restraint was applied
  • Duration of restraint use
  • Verification of the treating clinician’s order
  • Any alternative methods for managing the patient’s behavior that were attempted
  • Mental status and activity level
  • Patient’s level of pain before and following administration of analgesia, if appropriate
  • A description of the patient’s behavior that warranted bed rail restraint
  • Patient’s response to bed rail restraint
  • Details of the physical assessment, including skin integrity and circulation
  • The results of any assessment made following the application of physical restraint in a patient who is violent or exhibiting self-destructive behavior
  • The rationale for continuing or discontinuing the restraint
  • Frequency of observations/assessment following restraint application
  • Any unexpected patient events or outcomes, interventions performed, and if the treating clinician was notified
  • Patient/family education, including topics presented, response to education provided/discussed, plan for follow-up education, and details regarding any barriers to communication and/or techniques that promoted successful communication

Key Nursing Considerations

  • Restraints require a written order from the treating clinician, which cannot be written as a standing order, although manual restraint during an emergency medical procedure is often performed without a written order.
  • Restraints are applied in accordance with facility policy on restraint use, facility/unit protocol, and manufacturer instructions.
  • Preserving the patient’s dignity is essential when applying restraints
  • The patient must be protected from physical harm while restraints are in place
  • Review more about Complications associated with bed rail restraint use
  • There is a growing emphasis on the need for healthcare organizations to seek alternative methods of fall prevention
  • Review more about Alternatives to the use of physical restraints
  • The intent is to use the least amount of restraint possible while promoting patient safety and the safety of healthcare personnel

Nursing Outcomes

  • The patient is protected from harm when other interventions have proven ineffective or insufficient
  • Physical, psychological, and emotional complications sometimes associated with bed rail restraint use are avoided
  • Restraints are used in accordance with the treating clinician’s order and facility policy

Evidence

Research

In a systematic review of clinical studies, researchers determined that the widely held view that bed rails are inherently harmful was not adequately supported and suggested that instead of a universal reduction of bed rail use there should be a focus on eliminating outdated equipment and assessing appropriate bed rail use on a case-by-case basis.1

In a single-center study comparing RN and nursing assistant (NA) attitudes toward physical restraint use in older adult patients, researchers determined that both RNs and NAs reported that the most important reason to apply restraints was to prevent the patient from interfering with therapy (e.g., pulling out feeding tubes or I.V. catheters). NAs were more likely than RNs to state that physical restraints were important in preventing patient falls. 4

Guidelines

Use of bed rails is risky, especially if the patient is elderly or disoriented. The patient may be at risk for entrapment, entanglement, or falling and sustaining injuries. 1

Bed rail safety guidelines issued by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) Hospital Bed Safety Workgroup detail what must be considered in the design, maintenance, and placement of the bedrails and other parts of the bed.2

Secondary Information

Complications Associated with Bed Rail Restraint Use

Potential complications associated with the use of bedrails for patient restraint include:
  • Asphyxia
  • Strangulation
  • Death due to inability to escape from the building in the event of fire or other disaster
  • Falls or other injuries
  • Development and/or worsening of pressure ulcers
  • Decreased mobility
  • Agitation
  • Reduced bone mass
  • Stiffness
  • Frustration
  • Loss of dignity
  • Incontinence
  • Constipation

Alternatives to the Use of Physical Restraints

Interventions that can reduce or eliminate the need for physical restraints in older adults include:
  • Orienting patients to their environment and explaining all procedures
  • Providing companionship and supervision
  • Regular attention to toileting and other physical and personal needs
  • Evaluation of prescribed medications and performing routine pain assessment
  • Environmental design that allows for close observation by staff
  • Promoting patient relaxation by creating a relaxing and comfortable environment (e.g., minimizing noise, offering soothing music, using appropriate lighting, performing patient massage, providing movement activities)
  • Use of bed and chair alarms to alert staff when a patient needs assistance
  • Placement of confused patients in rooms that are close to the nurses’ station
  • Use of door alarms to reduce patient wandering

Patients who are at increased risk for intentionally harming others might be placed in seclusion instead of, or in addition to, having physical restraints applied.

Bed Rail Safety Guidelines

Ongoing close attention must considered in the design, placement, and maintenance of bedrails and other parts of the bed:
  • Bedrail bars should be appropriately spaced to prevent the patient’s head from passing through the opening and becoming trapped
  • The mattress should be correctly placed to prevent the patient from falling between the mattress and the bedrails
  • Check the mattress for shrinking and compression of the outside perimeter which would increase the space between the mattress and the bedrails
  • Check the mattress is the correct size for the bed
  • Fill the spaces between the bedrails and the mattress and the headboard with a firm inlay or a mattress
  • Latches securing bedrails should be stable
  • Older bedrail designs that have tapered or winged ends should not be used with patients at risk for entrapment
  • Ensure that patient needs (e.g., toileting, pain, comfort) are attended to at all times while the bed rails are up

Considerations for Involving the Clinician

  • If the restraint is applied in an emergent situation without a written order from the treating clinician, the treating clinician must perform an in-person assessment of the patient within 1 hour
  • The treating clinician’s assessment should include the patient’s need for bed rail restraint, the patient’s reaction to bed rail restraint, the patient’s behavioral and medical condition, and the need to continue or discontinue bed rail restraint
  • If the assessment is performed by an RN, the RN must consult with the treating clinician as soon as possible following the assessment
  • Each order for physical restraint must be renewed every 4 hours for adult patients. Orders can be renewed for up to 24 hours, at which point the treating clinician must assess the patient in person before renewing an order

Ongoing Assessment

  • Release the bed rail restraints every 2 hours (or according to the treating clinician’s order) for 15 minutes in order to assist the patient to the bathroom, offer food and fluids, assess the skin and circulation, and perform range of motion exercises
  • Assess the patient frequently while restraints are in use. Evaluate for signs of injury every 15 minutes or according to facility protocol for the type of restraint that is used
  • Observe I.V. catheters, urinary catheters, and drainage tubes at regular intervals to verify that they are positioned correctly and are not occluded by the bed rail restraint
  • Verify that the treating clinician performs a face-to-face evaluation of the patient within an appropriate period of time according to the facility/unit protocol. Depending on the conditions surrounding restraint use, this evaluation can be performed daily thereafter

Applying Bed Rail Restraints in Older Adults

Pre-Procedure

  6. Verify completion of facility informed consent documents pertaining to restraint use
  7. Check the orders and care plan
  8. Review the manufacturer’s instructions for all supplies to be used, and verify that they are in good working order
  9. Maintain level of infection precautions, as appropriate
  10. Provide privacy for the patient
  11. Identify the patient
  12. Introduce yourself

  3. Fill the space between the bed rails and mattress and between the headboard and mattress with blankets or pillows, to prevent the patient from falling into the space. Older adults who are physically small and patients who have agitation or delirium may be at increased risk for entrapment

Post-Procedure

  1. Evaluate the effectiveness of the restraint in appropriately curtailing the patient’s movement
  2. Assess the patient’s overall comfort level, including if he or she is in pain and if he or she is breathing comfortably
  3. Ask the patient directly if the patient can communicate, or ask the opinion of family members, if present. Use a facility-approved pain assessment tool appropriate for the patient
  4. In general, a treating clinician must make a face-to-face assessment of the patient and his or her condition within 1 hour of application of the restraint.
  5. The appropriateness of restraint use is serially evaluated. The patient’s activity level and behavior are monitored such that clinicians can assess if conditions that made restraint necessary are still present.
  6. Maintain safety of the patient
  7. Ask the patient if any needs are unmet
  8. State the time of your expected return
  9. Properly dispose of standard precaution PPE
  10. Clean the equipment if there has been contact with the patient (see specific manufacturer instructions)
  11. Perform hand hygiene
  12. Secure the nurse call light within easy reach of the patient, as appropriate
  13. Leave the bed or chair with the wheels locked
  14. Place the bed in the lowest position if the patient is in bed

Communication Techniques with Patients

  • Allow extra time when talking with older patients because they typically require more time to process information and to communicate
  • Decrease environmental distractions to improve older patients’ ability to receive and understand information, including the amount of visual and auditory distractions
  • Sit facing the patient to help the patient focus on what is being said. This can be especially helpful for patients with vision or hearing loss
  • Maintain eye contact
  • Listen carefully without interrupting
  • Speak slowly, clearly, and loudly
  • Use short, simple words and sentences
  • Use pictures to help the patient and family understand what bed rails look like, how they are applied, and how they control certain movements
  • Give the patient and family members ample opportunity to ask questions
  • Do not appear rushed or uninterested
